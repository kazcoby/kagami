Answer Key with Explanations

(For instructor use — not shown to students until after submission)

Question 1: B. To hold water that blocks sewer gases from entering your home

The curved shape of the P-trap holds water, creating a seal that prevents sewer gases from rising up through your drains. If a sink you rarely use starts smelling bad, run water for 30 seconds to refill the trap.

Question 2: C. Worn flapper

The flapper is a rubber seal at the bottom of the toilet tank. Over time, it wears out, warps, or develops mineral deposits, preventing it from sealing properly. This allows water to constantly leak from the tank into the bowl, causing the "running" sound as the fill valve tries to keep up.

Question 3: B. Mix different drain cleaning products together

Mixing drain cleaning chemicals can create toxic gases including chlorine gas, which is extremely dangerous. This is also why you should always tell a plumber if you've used chemical cleaners before they work on your drain.

Question 4: B. Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter

A GFCI monitors electricity flowing through a circuit and interrupts (cuts) power if it detects a ground fault—electricity going somewhere it shouldn't, like through water or a person. It can cut power in 1/40th of a second.

Question 5: C. Monthly

GFCIs should be tested monthly by pressing the TEST button (power should cut off) and then the RESET button (power should restore). If either step fails, the GFCI needs to be replaced. Regular testing ensures they'll work when needed.

Question 6: B. 30-50%

Relative humidity between 30-50% is the ideal range. Below 30% can cause dry skin, static electricity, and cracked wood. Above 60% creates conditions favorable for mold growth, dust mites, and musty odors.

Question 7: A. Within 24-48 hours

Mold can begin growing within 24-48 hours of water exposure, which is why it's critical to address leaks and dry wet areas immediately. After a leak, increasing ventilation and using fans or dehumidifiers can help prevent mold growth.

Question 8: C. Multiple drains clogged at the same time

When multiple drains are clogged simultaneously, it typically indicates a problem with the main sewer line rather than individual fixture clogs. This requires professional equipment and expertise to diagnose and repair. A single clogged sink, running toilet, or tripped GFCI are typically DIY-appropriate issues.
