Page 1: Your Plumbing System Overview

Understanding how water flows through your home helps you diagnose problems and communicate with plumbers. Your plumbing system has two main parts: supply (water in) and drainage (water out).

The Supply Side: Water In

How Water Gets to Your Fixtures

  1. Water main: Brings water from the city supply (or well) to your property
  2. Main shutoff: Controls all water entering your home
  3. Water meter: Measures usage (useful for detecting hidden leaks)
  4. Water heater: Heats water for one branch of the supply
  5. Supply lines: Hot and cold pipes run to each fixture
  6. Fixture shutoffs: Individual valves at each sink, toilet, etc.
Water Pressure: Normal home water pressure is 40-60 PSI. Too high (>80 PSI) can damage pipes and appliances. Too low makes showers frustrating. Sudden pressure changes may indicate a problem.

The Drain Side: Water Out

How Wastewater Leaves

  1. Drain: The visible opening where water enters (sink, tub, toilet)
  2. P-trap: The curved pipe under sinks—holds water to block sewer gases
  3. Drain lines: Carry water to the main sewer line
  4. Vent stack: Pipe going through the roof—allows air in so water drains properly
  5. Main sewer line: Carries all wastewater to city sewer or septic tank

Key Things to Know

The P-Trap

That curved pipe under every sink serves a vital purpose: it holds water that blocks sewer gases from entering your home. If a sink you rarely use starts smelling bad, run water for 30 seconds to refill the trap.

Clean-Outs

These are access points for clearing clogs in main drain lines. They look like capped pipes, often found in basements, crawl spaces, or outside. Know where yours are—plumbers will ask!

Water Heater Basics

  • Tank: Stores 30-80 gallons of hot water, heats continuously
  • Tankless: Heats water on-demand, no storage
  • Typical lifespan: 8-12 years (tank), 15-20 years (tankless)
  • Location: Basement, garage, utility closet, or sometimes attic
Stop and Call a Pro If...
  • You smell sewer gas persistently (dangerous!)
  • Multiple drains are clogged simultaneously (main line issue)
  • You see water stains on walls/ceilings without an obvious source
  • Water pressure suddenly drops significantly
  • Your water heater is leaking from the tank itself

Reading Your Water Meter

Your water meter can help detect hidden leaks:

  1. Make sure no water is running (don't flush, run dishwasher, etc.)
  2. Read the meter or check the flow indicator (small triangle or dial)
  3. Wait 2 hours with no water use
  4. Read again—if it changed, you have a leak somewhere

Action Items

  1. Locate your water heater
  2. Find the P-trap under a bathroom sink (open the cabinet and look)
  3. If you have a basement, look for the main drain clean-out
  4. Note the age of your water heater (often on a sticker)
