Page 5: Leak Detection and Water Damage Prevention

Water damage is sneaky. A slow leak can go unnoticed for months, causing thousands of dollars in damage and creating conditions for mold growth. Regular inspection is your best defense.

The Cost of Ignoring Leaks
  • Water damage is the #2 most common home insurance claim
  • Average claim: $11,000+
  • Mold remediation can cost $10,000-30,000
  • Many policies DON'T cover gradual leaks or mold

Prevention and early detection are critical!

Signs of Water Damage

Visual Signs

  • Water stains on ceilings or walls (often yellow/brown rings)
  • Bubbling, peeling, or warped paint
  • Warped or buckling flooring
  • Visible mold or mildew (black, green, or white spots)
  • Efflorescence (white mineral deposits on concrete/brick)
  • Rust stains around fixtures or pipes

Other Signs

  • Musty or earthy smell (often the first sign of hidden moisture)
  • Unexplained increase in water bill
  • Sound of running water when nothing is on
  • Warm spots on the floor (hot water line leak)
  • Decreased water pressure

Regular Inspection Areas

Under Sinks (Check Monthly)

  • Look at the P-trap and all connections
  • Check supply line connections (hot and cold)
  • Look for water stains, mineral deposits, or corrosion
  • Feel for dampness on the cabinet floor
  • Check garbage disposal connections (kitchen)

Around Toilets (Check Monthly)

  • Feel the floor around the toilet base—any soft spots or dampness?
  • Check the supply line connection at the wall
  • Look for water stains at the base
  • Does the toilet rock? (May indicate wax ring failure)

Water Heater (Check Quarterly)

  • Look for rust or water at the base
  • Check the pressure relief valve (T&P valve) pipe—should be dry
  • Look for corrosion on connections
  • Note the age (sticker on unit)—plan replacement after 10 years

Washing Machine (Check Monthly)

  • Inspect supply hoses for bulges, cracks, or rust
  • Replace rubber hoses every 5 years (or upgrade to braided steel)
  • Check the floor behind/under the machine
  • Ensure the drain hose is secure

Dishwasher (Check Monthly)

  • Look under and around the unit for moisture
  • Check door gasket for wear
  • Ensure drain connection is secure

Refrigerator with Ice Maker (Check Quarterly)

  • Pull out and check the water supply line
  • Look for water stains on the floor
  • Check drip pan under the unit

The Water Meter Test

Use your water meter to detect hidden leaks:

  1. Turn off ALL water in the house (don't run dishwasher, washing machine, etc.)
  2. Locate your water meter (usually near the street or in the basement)
  3. Record the reading or note the position of the flow indicator (small triangle or dial)
  4. Don't use any water for 2 hours
  5. Check the meter again
  6. If it changed, you have a leak somewhere
Smart Water Monitors

Devices like Flo, Moen Flo, or Phyn can be installed on your main water line. They detect leaks, unusual usage, and can automatically shut off water if a pipe bursts. Many insurance companies offer discounts for having them installed.

Renters: Point leak detectors ($15-50) can be placed under sinks and near water heaters to alert you to leaks.

Immediate Response to Leaks

Leak Type Immediate Action
Under sink Turn off fixture shutoffs under the sink
Toilet Turn off supply valve behind toilet
Water heater Turn off water supply to heater + call pro
Washing machine Turn off supply valves behind machine
Ceiling leak Place bucket, poke hole to drain if bulging, find source above
Unknown source Turn off main water shutoff

After a Leak: Preventing Mold

Mold can begin growing within 24-48 hours of water exposure.

  1. Remove standing water — Mop, wet-vac, or towels
  2. Increase air circulation — Open windows, run fans
  3. Run dehumidifier — Target below 50% humidity
  4. Remove wet materials — Soaked rugs, boxes, papers
  5. Document everything — Photos and videos for insurance
  6. Call insurance — Report promptly; they may send a restoration company
Stop and Call a Pro If...
  • Water is coming through the ceiling (could be pipe or roof)
  • You see or smell mold larger than a few square feet
  • Water damage affects more than a small area
  • The leak is behind a wall or in the foundation
  • You can't identify or access the source
  • The water is sewage or contaminated

Renter vs. Homeowner

Renters:

  • Report leaks to landlord IMMEDIATELY in writing (email creates a record)
  • Take photos before and after reporting
  • You're typically not responsible for repairs, but you ARE responsible for reporting promptly
  • Get renter's insurance—it covers YOUR belongings if damaged by water

Homeowners:

  • You're responsible for detection, repair, and damage costs
  • Know what your homeowner's insurance covers (sudden vs. gradual)
  • Consider a home warranty that covers plumbing
  • Budget for potential repairs—water heater replacement averages $1,000-3,000

Action Items

  1. Do a visual inspection under all sinks today
  2. Check around your toilets for soft spots or stains
  3. Perform the water meter test this week
  4. Consider adding leak detectors in high-risk areas
  5. Review your insurance policy's water damage coverage
