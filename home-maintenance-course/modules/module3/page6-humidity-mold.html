Page 6: Humidity Control and Mold Prevention

Mold needs three things to grow: moisture, warmth, and organic material (like drywall, wood, or dust). You can't eliminate warmth or organic material, so controlling moisture is your primary defense.

Understanding Humidity

What is Relative Humidity?

Relative humidity (RH) measures how much water vapor is in the air compared to how much it could hold at that temperature. It's expressed as a percentage.

  • Below 30%: Too dry—can cause dry skin, static electricity, cracked wood
  • 30-50%: Ideal range for comfort and health
  • Above 60%: Too humid—promotes mold, dust mites, and musty smells
Get a Hygrometer!

A digital hygrometer ($10-20) tells you the humidity level in your home. Place one in a problem area (bathroom, basement, or near windows) to monitor moisture levels. Some smart thermostats also display humidity.

Sources of Indoor Humidity

Source Moisture Added Daily
Showering/bathing 0.5+ pints per shower
Cooking 0.5-1 pint
Breathing (per person) 0.5-1 pint
Indoor drying clothes 4-6 pints per load
Houseplants 0.5-1 pint (many plants)
Unvented gas appliances Variable (significant)

Signs of High Humidity

  • Condensation on windows (especially in cold weather)
  • Musty or damp smell
  • Peeling wallpaper or bubbling paint
  • Foggy mirrors that take a long time to clear
  • Visible mold or mildew (black spots, fuzzy growth)
  • Warped wood (doors sticking, floor cupping)
  • Rust on metal fixtures

Controlling Humidity

Use Exhaust Fans Properly

  • Run bathroom fan DURING and 15-20 minutes AFTER showering
  • Run kitchen range hood when cooking, especially with steam
  • Ensure fans are actually vented outside (not into attic)
  • Clean fan covers regularly (dusty fans don't move air well)
Tip: Don't have a bathroom fan? Open a window slightly during and after showers. Consider asking your landlord to install one, or use a small portable fan to improve air circulation.

Improve Ventilation

  • Open windows when weather permits (cross-ventilation is best)
  • Don't block air vents with furniture
  • Keep closet doors and cabinet doors open occasionally
  • Ensure dryer vents outside (never inside)

Use a Dehumidifier

For basements, humid climates, or persistent moisture:

  • Set to maintain 45-50% humidity
  • Empty the tank regularly (or use continuous drain)
  • Clean the filter monthly
  • Size appropriately for the space (check square footage rating)

Address Specific Issues

  • Window condensation: Improve ventilation, consider storm windows, or lower humidity
  • Damp basement: Check for water entry points, improve drainage, use dehumidifier
  • Crawl space: Ensure vapor barrier is in place; may need professional assessment
  • A/C drip: Ensure condensate drain line is clear

Mold Prevention Best Practices

  1. Fix leaks within 24-48 hours — Mold can start growing in 24 hours
  2. Dry wet areas immediately — Don't leave wet towels on the floor or spills unattended
  3. Keep surfaces clean — Mold feeds on dust and dirt
  4. Use mold-resistant products — In bathrooms, use mold-resistant caulk and paint
  5. Don't carpet bathrooms or basements — Hard flooring is better for wet areas
  6. Clean shower/tub weekly — Soap scum feeds mold
  7. Spread out shower curtain to dry — Don't leave it bunched up

When You See Mold

Small Mold (Less Than 10 Square Feet)

You can typically clean small areas of mold yourself:

  1. Wear gloves, eye protection, and an N95 mask
  2. Mix solution: 1 cup bleach to 1 gallon water (or use commercial mold cleaner)
  3. Scrub the affected area
  4. Let sit for 15 minutes, then wipe clean
  5. Dry thoroughly
  6. Address the moisture source to prevent return
DO NOT Attempt Mold Removal If:
  • The area is larger than 10 square feet (roughly 3x3 feet)
  • Mold is in HVAC systems or ductwork
  • Mold is caused by sewage or contaminated water
  • Anyone in the home has mold allergies, asthma, or immune issues
  • Mold is behind walls or in hidden areas
  • You're not sure what you're dealing with

Professional mold remediation is required for these situations.

Stop and Call a Pro If...
  • Mold covers an area larger than 10 square feet
  • There's a strong musty smell but you can't find the source
  • Mold keeps returning despite cleaning
  • You or family members have mold-related health symptoms
  • Water damage was caused by flooding or sewage
  • Mold is in your HVAC system

Renter vs. Homeowner

Renters:

  • Report mold or moisture problems to your landlord immediately (in writing)
  • Document everything with dated photos
  • Landlords are typically responsible for mold caused by building issues
  • YOU are responsible for moisture you create (not running exhaust fans, etc.)
  • If landlord doesn't respond to mold issues, know your local tenant rights

Homeowners:

  • You're responsible for all mold prevention and remediation
  • Most homeowner's insurance does NOT cover mold unless caused by a covered peril
  • Professional mold inspection: $200-600
  • Professional remediation: $500-30,000+ depending on extent

Action Items

  1. Check humidity level in your home (get a hygrometer if needed)
  2. Inspect for signs of mold in bathrooms, under sinks, and in basement
  3. Make sure you're using exhaust fans properly
  4. If humidity is above 60%, take steps to reduce it
  5. Clean any small mold patches you find
