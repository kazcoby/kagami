Page 4: Smoke Detectors and CO Alarms

Smoke detectors and carbon monoxide (CO) alarms are your first line of defense against two invisible killers. Properly installed and maintained, they give you the precious minutes needed to escape a fire or CO poisoning situation.

Understanding the Difference

Smoke Detector CO Alarm
Detects smoke particles in the air Detects carbon monoxide gas
Warns of fire Warns of combustion byproduct (odorless, colorless)
Install on ceilings or high on walls Install at any height (CO mixes with air)
Typically 3 short beeps = alarm Typically 4 short beeps = alarm
Replace every 10 years Replace every 5-7 years
Combination Units: Many modern devices combine smoke and CO detection in one unit. These are convenient, but remember they have different replacement schedules—the CO sensor may expire before the smoke sensor.

Where to Install Them

Smoke Detectors

  • Inside each bedroom
  • Outside each sleeping area (in the hallway)
  • On every level of the home, including basement
  • In the kitchen (but not directly over the stove—10 feet away minimum)
  • Near the top of stairways

CO Alarms

  • On every level of the home
  • Near each sleeping area (within 10 feet of bedroom doors)
  • Near attached garages
  • Near fuel-burning appliances (but not directly next to them)

The Dreaded Chirp: What Different Sounds Mean

Continuous Alarm (3-4 beeps, repeating)

This is an emergency. The device is detecting smoke or CO.

  • For smoke: Get out, stay out, call 911
  • For CO: Get everyone outside to fresh air, call 911, don't re-enter

Single Chirp Every 30-60 Seconds

Low battery warning. Replace the battery (see instructions below).

Chirping That Won't Stop After Battery Change

End of life warning. The entire unit needs replacement (usually after 7-10 years).

5 Beeps Every Minute (CO alarms)

End of life warning. Replace the CO alarm immediately.

How to Replace a Battery

  1. Remove the detector from its mount. Most twist off counterclockwise, some have a release tab.
  2. Open the battery compartment. Usually a sliding door or the entire back panel.
  3. Remove the old battery and note its type (usually 9V or AA).
  4. Insert the new battery matching the + and - symbols.
  5. Close the compartment and remount the detector.
  6. Press the test button to confirm it's working (you'll hear the alarm).
Pro Tip: Use the time change (spring forward/fall back) as your reminder to test all alarms and replace batteries. Even "10-year sealed battery" units should be tested regularly.

Monthly Testing

  1. Press and hold the test button on each detector
  2. The alarm should sound loudly
  3. If the sound is weak or absent, replace batteries first, then the unit if needed
  4. Make sure everyone in the home knows what the alarm sounds like

When to Replace the Entire Unit

  • Smoke detectors: Every 10 years (check the date on the back)
  • CO alarms: Every 5-7 years (check the date)
  • After any alarm that was caused by actual smoke or CO
  • If the unit fails the test button check even with new batteries
  • If it's yellowed, dirty, or physically damaged
RENTERS: Your landlord is legally required to provide working smoke detectors (and CO alarms in most states). However:
  • Battery replacement is typically YOUR responsibility
  • If a unit is hardwired and fails, report it immediately in writing
  • Document all alarms in your unit with photos (date on back visible)
  • You can add additional battery-powered units at your own expense
HOMEOWNERS: Consider upgrading to interconnected alarms—when one sounds, they all sound. This is especially important in larger homes. Many jurisdictions now require this for new construction or when selling.
NEVER:
  • Remove batteries to stop a nuisance alarm (relocate the detector instead)
  • Paint over a smoke detector
  • Ignore a chirping alarm "for now"
  • Assume hardwired units don't need battery backup replacement

Action Items

  1. Count your smoke detectors and CO alarms
  2. Test each one using the test button
  3. Check the manufacture date on the back of each unit
  4. Note any that need battery replacement or full replacement
  5. Add to your Home Systems Checklist
