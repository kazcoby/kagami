Page 1: Building a Basic Tool Kit

You don't need a garage full of tools to handle basic home maintenance. A well-chosen starter kit will cover 90% of what you'll encounter. Here's what to get—and what can wait.

Quality Over Quantity

A few good-quality tools will serve you better than many cheap ones. Cheap screwdrivers strip; cheap pliers slip. For items you'll use often (screwdrivers, tape measure), invest a bit more.

Tier 1: The Essentials

Start here. These tools handle most basic tasks.

Screwdrivers

  • Phillips head (the + shape): #1 and #2 sizes cover most screws
  • Flathead (the - shape): Small and medium sizes
  • Or: A 6-in-1 or multi-bit screwdriver ($8-15)

Use for: Tightening cabinet hardware, outlet covers, door hinges, furniture assembly

Pliers

  • Slip-joint pliers: Adjustable jaw for gripping various sizes
  • Needle-nose pliers: For tight spaces and precision work

$10-20 for a basic set

Use for: Pulling nails, gripping pipes, bending wire, holding small parts

Hammer

  • 16 oz claw hammer: The "claw" removes nails; the head drives them
  • Look for a comfortable grip

$10-20

Use for: Hanging pictures, light demolition, assembling furniture, removing nails

Tape Measure

  • 16-25 foot tape measure: Wide blade (1") stays rigid when extended
  • Locking feature is essential

$5-15

Use for: Measuring furniture, hanging items level, buying replacement parts

Adjustable Wrench

  • 8-10 inch adjustable wrench: One wrench fits many nut/bolt sizes
  • Also called a "Crescent wrench"

$10-15

Use for: Tightening plumbing connections, assembling furniture, any bolts

Flashlight

  • LED flashlight or headlamp
  • Headlamp keeps hands free

$10-25

Use for: Seeing under sinks, in attics, during power outages, in closets

Utility Knife

  • Retractable blade for safety
  • Extra blades (they dull quickly)

$5-10

Use for: Opening packages, cutting drywall, trimming caulk, scoring material

Tier 2: Very Useful Additions

Add these when you can. They make many jobs easier.

Level

  • 9-12 inch torpedo level: Compact, fits in tight spaces
  • Or use a smartphone level app in a pinch

$5-15

Use for: Hanging pictures straight, checking if shelves are level

Stud Finder

  • Electronic or magnetic
  • Essential for hanging heavy items securely

$15-40

Use for: Finding wall studs for mounting TVs, heavy shelves, grab bars

Cordless Drill

  • 12V is enough for most home tasks
  • Look for: drill + screwdriver bits, variable speed, battery included

$40-80

Use for: Driving screws quickly, drilling pilot holes, assembling furniture

Putty Knife Set

  • 1.5" for detail work
  • 3-4" for larger areas
  • Flexible blade for spreading; stiff for scraping

$5-10 for set

Use for: Patching drywall, spreading spackle, scraping paint

Caulk Gun

  • Basic ratchet style works fine
  • Smooth rod (not ratchet) is easier for control

$5-15

Use for: Applying caulk around tubs, sinks, windows

Tier 3: Nice to Have

Expand your collection as specific needs arise.

  • Allen key set (hex wrenches) — For IKEA-style furniture, some fixtures
  • Voltage tester — Confirms power is off before working near wiring
  • Duct tape — Temporary fixes (emphasis on temporary!)
  • Plumber's tape — Seals threaded pipe connections
  • WD-40 or silicone lubricant — Squeaky hinges, stuck mechanisms
  • Assorted screws and nails — Small kit saves hardware store trips
  • Safety glasses — Protect eyes when drilling, hammering, or working overhead
  • Work gloves — Protect hands from splinters, sharp edges

Storage Tips

  • Tool bag or box: Keeps everything in one place and portable
  • Drawer organizer: Great for apartment dwellers with limited space
  • Pegboard: Visual storage if you have garage/workshop space
  • Keep it accessible: Tools you can't find don't help
Renter Tip: A small tool bag under the sink or in a closet is plenty. You don't need a dedicated workshop—just enough tools to handle quick fixes and emergencies.
Tool Safety Reminders
  • Keep tools clean and dry to prevent rust
  • Store sharp tools safely (blades covered/retracted)
  • Use the right tool for the job—wrong tools cause injuries
  • Wear safety glasses when there's any risk of flying debris
  • If you're unsure how to use a tool safely, look it up first

Action Items

  1. Take inventory: What tools do you already have?
  2. Identify gaps: Which Tier 1 essentials are you missing?
  3. Make a shopping list for your next hardware store visit
  4. Designate a home for your tools (even just a drawer or bag)
