Page 2: Patching Drywall (Small Holes)

Nail holes, doorknob dings, and small accidents leave marks on walls. The good news: patching drywall is one of the easiest home repairs to learn. The key is matching your method to the hole size.

Renter Alert!

Repairing small holes before you move out can save significant money from your security deposit. Landlords often charge $25-50+ per hole for professional repair. A tube of spackle costs $5.

Choose Your Method by Hole Size

Hole Size Examples Method
Tiny (under 1/4") Nail holes, small tack holes Spackle only
Small (1/4" to 1") Anchor holes, small dings Spackle with multiple coats
Medium (1" to 3") Doorknob impacts, larger anchors Self-adhesive mesh patch + spackle
Large (3" to 6") Fist-sized holes, big accidents Drywall patch kit
Very Large (6"+) Major damage Call a professional

Supplies You'll Need

  • Spackle (lightweight or all-purpose) — $5-8
  • Putty knife (1.5" and/or 3") — $5-10
  • Fine sandpaper (120-150 grit) or sanding sponge — $3-5
  • Self-adhesive mesh patches (for medium holes) — $5-10
  • Primer (optional but recommended) — $5-10
  • Matching paint — varies
  • Damp cloth or sponge

Method 1: Tiny Holes (Nail/Tack Holes)

Time: 5 minutes + drying time

Difficulty: Very Easy

1Clean the hole: Remove any loose debris. If there's a nail, remove it.

2Apply spackle: Put a small amount on your finger or putty knife. Press into the hole.

3Smooth it: Wipe across the surface to remove excess. It should be nearly flush.

4Let dry: Usually 15-30 minutes (spackle turns from dark to light when dry).

5Light sand if needed: Only if there's a bump. Wipe dust.

6Touch up paint: Small dab with a brush or even a cotton swab.

Method 2: Small to Medium Holes (Up to 3")

Time: 30-45 minutes total (multiple drying periods)

Difficulty: Easy

1Clean and prep: Remove loose drywall pieces. For holes 1"+, apply a self-adhesive mesh patch over the hole.

2First coat: Apply spackle over the patch/hole using your putty knife. Spread beyond the hole edges. Don't worry about perfection.

3Let dry completely: Follow product directions (usually 1-2 hours). Spackle shrinks as it dries!

4Second coat: Apply another thin layer, feathering the edges outward. This fills shrinkage and blends edges.

5Let dry: Another 1-2 hours or overnight.

6Sand smooth: Use fine sandpaper in circular motions. The goal is a smooth surface flush with the wall. Wipe dust with damp cloth.

7Prime (recommended): Bare spackle absorbs paint differently. A light coat of primer helps paint match.

8Paint: Match your wall color. Multiple thin coats blend better than one thick coat.

Pro Tips for Invisible Repairs

  • Feather the edges: Blend spackle outward gradually. Abrupt edges show through paint.
  • Multiple thin coats beat one thick coat: Thick spackle cracks and shrinks more.
  • Match the texture: If your walls have texture (orange peel, knockdown), you may need texture spray.
  • Paint matching: If you don't have the original paint, take a chip to the hardware store for color matching. Or repaint the entire wall for seamless results.
  • Check in different lighting: Imperfections often show at angles or in raking light.
Texture Tip: If your walls have orange peel or knockdown texture, home improvement stores sell spray-on texture in cans ($8-15). Practice on cardboard first—it's tricky to match!

What About Larger Holes?

For holes 3-6 inches, you'll need a drywall patch kit that includes:

  • A metal or plastic backing plate
  • Or a "California patch" technique (drywall piece with paper border)

These are DIY-able but require more skill. YouTube tutorials are helpful for these repairs.

Stop and Call a Pro If...
  • The hole is larger than 6 inches
  • There's water damage (need to address the source first)
  • You see mold behind the drywall
  • The damage involves multiple holes or structural concerns
  • The wall contains electrical wiring or plumbing
  • You're repairing a ceiling (harder and potentially structural)

Renter vs. Homeowner

Renters:

  • Small nail holes from hanging pictures are usually considered normal wear
  • Larger holes should be repaired before move-out
  • Keep your paint colors! Ask your landlord for touch-up paint, or note the paint code
  • If unsure what's allowed, ask before drilling or mounting anything

Homeowners:

  • Keep leftover paint labeled and stored for touch-ups
  • Address holes before repainting walls (much easier!)
  • Document repairs if planning to sell—shows home care

Action Items

  1. Survey your walls for holes that need patching
  2. Categorize them by size (tiny, small, medium)
  3. Purchase supplies for your specific needs
  4. Practice on a tiny hole first before tackling larger repairs
