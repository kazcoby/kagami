Page 6: Working with Landlords and Pros

Knowing how to communicate effectively with landlords and contractors saves money, reduces stress, and gets problems fixed faster. This page covers the communication skills that make you an effective advocate for your home.

For Renters: Communicating with Your Landlord

The Golden Rule: Document Everything

  • Always submit maintenance requests in writing (email preferred)
  • Keep copies of all correspondence
  • Take dated photos of problems before and after
  • Follow up phone calls with a summary email ("Per our conversation today...")
Why Written Requests Matter:

Written requests create a paper trail. If your landlord fails to make required repairs, this documentation is essential for any legal recourse. It also ensures nothing falls through the cracks.

Effective Maintenance Request Template

Subject: Maintenance Request - [Issue] at [Unit/Address]

Dear [Landlord/Property Manager],

I am writing to report [brief description of issue] at [address/unit].

Details:

  • Location: [Specific room/area]
  • When noticed: [Date]
  • Description: [Detailed description of problem]
  • Urgency: [Emergency/Urgent/Routine]

[If applicable: "I have attached photos for reference."]

Please let me know when someone can address this issue. My availability is [days/times].

Thank you,
[Your name]
[Phone number]
[Unit address]

Priority Levels

Level Examples Expected Response
Emergency Gas leak, no heat in winter, flooding, fire damage, no electricity, broken locks Same day (often 24 hours or less required by law)
Urgent No hot water, refrigerator broken, A/C out in extreme heat, plumbing leak 1-3 days
Routine Running toilet, squeaky door, minor appliance issues 1-2 weeks
Non-urgent Cosmetic issues, minor inconveniences 2-4 weeks or next available

If Your Landlord Doesn't Respond

  1. Follow up in writing: Reference original request, note time passed
  2. Send via certified mail: Creates proof of receipt
  3. Know your rights: Look up your state/local tenant rights
  4. Contact housing authority: For habitability issues
  5. Consider legal aid: Free tenant legal clinics exist in many areas
What NOT to Do
  • Don't withhold rent without understanding your state's laws
  • Don't make major repairs yourself without permission (and expect reimbursement)
  • Don't let issues go unreported—you may be blamed for resulting damage

For Everyone: Finding and Working with Contractors

Finding Quality Professionals

  • Ask for referrals: Friends, neighbors, family, coworkers
  • Check reviews: Google, Yelp, Nextdoor, Angi (formerly Angie's List)
  • Verify licensing: Look up contractor licenses on your state's website
  • Check insurance: Ask for proof of liability and workers' comp
  • Get multiple quotes: At least 3 for major work
Red Flags to Watch For:
  • No written estimate or contract
  • Demands full payment upfront
  • No physical business address
  • Pressure to decide immediately
  • Can't provide references
  • Significantly cheaper than all other quotes
  • "Cash only" requirement

Getting Good Estimates

When requesting quotes:

  1. Describe the problem clearly (use what you learned in this course!)
  2. Ask for written estimates
  3. Get breakdown of materials vs. labor
  4. Ask about timeline and scheduling
  5. Clarify what's included (cleanup, disposal, permits?)
  6. Ask about warranty on work

Questions to Ask Contractors

  • How long have you been doing this type of work?
  • Are you licensed and insured? (Verify!)
  • Can you provide references from recent similar jobs?
  • What's your typical timeline for this project?
  • Who will actually be doing the work?
  • How do you handle unexpected issues or changes?
  • What's your payment schedule?
  • Do you pull necessary permits?

The Written Contract

For any significant work, get a written contract including:

  • Detailed scope of work
  • Materials to be used (brand, quality level)
  • Total price and payment schedule
  • Start and completion dates
  • Who obtains permits
  • Change order process
  • Warranty information
  • Cleanup and debris removal
Payment Best Practices:
  • Never pay 100% upfront
  • Typical: 1/3 deposit, 1/3 midpoint, 1/3 on completion
  • Final payment only after walkthrough and satisfaction
  • Pay by check or credit card (creates paper trail)

Describing Problems Effectively

What you've learned in this course helps you communicate better with professionals:

Instead of: "Something is wrong with my toilet."

Say: "My toilet runs intermittently. I did the dye test and saw color in the bowl after 15 minutes, so I think the flapper is leaking. I checked the water level and it's about 1 inch below the overflow tube."

Instead of: "My air isn't working."

Say: "My A/C is running but not cooling well. The air coming from vents feels lukewarm. I checked the filter yesterday and replaced it. The outdoor unit is running. Thermostat is set to 72° but inside temperature is 78°."

Information to Have Ready

  • Specific location of problem
  • When it started and how often it occurs
  • What you've already tried
  • Age of equipment (if relevant)
  • Brand and model numbers
  • Recent changes (new appliances, work done, etc.)
  • Photos or videos of the issue
