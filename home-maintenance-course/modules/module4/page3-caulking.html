Page 3: Caulking Like a Pro

Caulk is the flexible sealant that fills gaps around tubs, sinks, windows, and more. When it cracks, peels, or develops mold, water can seep behind surfaces causing damage. Re-caulking is a simple skill that prevents expensive repairs.

Why Caulk Matters:

Failed caulk around a bathtub can let water seep behind tiles and into walls. Over time, this causes rot, mold, and structural damage. A $5 tube of caulk prevents thousands in repair costs.

When to Re-Caulk

  • Caulk is cracked, peeling, or pulling away
  • Visible gaps between surfaces
  • Mold or mildew that won't clean off (it's IN the caulk)
  • Caulk is discolored, hard, or brittle
  • Water appears to be getting behind the caulk line

Choosing the Right Caulk

Caulk Type Best For Notes
100% Silicone Tubs, showers, sinks, wet areas Waterproof, flexible, mold-resistant. Harder to apply but lasts longest. NOT paintable.
Silicone-Latex Blend Bathrooms, kitchens Easier to apply than pure silicone. Paintable. Good water resistance.
Acrylic Latex Interior trim, baseboards, windows (interior) Easy to apply and paint. Less flexible, not for wet areas.
Mold-Resistant Bathrooms, any damp area Contains anti-microbial additives. Worth the extra cost in bathrooms.
Color Options:

White and clear are most common. Clear works well where surfaces don't match. Some specialty caulks come in colors like almond, gray, or brown for tile matching.

Supplies Needed

  • Caulk (choose appropriate type)
  • Caulk gun ($5-15)
  • Utility knife or caulk removal tool
  • Painter's tape (optional but helpful)
  • Rubbing alcohol or caulk remover
  • Clean rags
  • Small container of water (for smoothing)
  • Latex gloves (silicone is sticky!)

Step-by-Step: Re-Caulking a Bathtub

This process works for tubs, showers, sinks, and similar applications.

Part 1: Removal and Prep

1Remove old caulk: Use a utility knife to cut along both edges of old caulk. Pull it out in strips. A plastic caulk removal tool helps scrape residue.

2Clean the surfaces: Remove all old caulk, soap scum, and mildew. Use rubbing alcohol or a mold-killing cleaner for stubborn areas.

3Let it dry completely: Caulk won't adhere to wet surfaces. Wait at least an hour, preferably overnight.

4Apply painter's tape (optional): Tape along both sides of the joint for clean, straight lines. Leave a gap the width you want the caulk bead.

Part 2: Applying New Caulk

5Prepare the tube: Cut the tip at a 45° angle. Start with a small opening—you can always cut more. Puncture the inner seal with a long nail or the tool on your caulk gun.

6Load the gun: Pull back the rod, insert the tube, and push the rod forward until it engages the plunger.

7Apply steadily: Hold the gun at a 45° angle. Squeeze the trigger while pulling (not pushing) the gun along the joint. Aim for a consistent bead that fills the gap.

8Release pressure: Press the release lever to stop caulk flow at the end of each run. This prevents over-application.

Part 3: Smoothing and Finishing

9Smooth the bead: Dip your finger in water (or use a caulk finishing tool) and run it along the caulk line in one smooth motion. This creates a professional concave finish.

10Remove tape immediately: If you used painter's tape, pull it off while caulk is still wet. Pull at an angle away from the caulk line.

11Clean up: Wipe excess caulk with a damp rag. Clean tools immediately—dried caulk is hard to remove.

12Let cure: Don't use the tub/sink for 24-48 hours (check product label). Silicone needs time to fully cure.

Pro Tips for Perfect Caulk Lines

  • Fill the tub first: When caulking around a bathtub, fill it with water before applying. The weight settles the tub, and the caulk cures in the "used" position.
  • Work in sections: Don't try to do the entire tub at once. Do one side, smooth it, then move on.
  • Less is more: A thin, consistent bead looks better than thick globs. You can always add more.
  • 45° angle matters: Cutting the tip at an angle gives you control and lets you see where you're applying.
  • Practice first: Lay a bead on cardboard or newspaper to get a feel for the gun before starting your actual project.

Common Caulking Locations

  • Bathtub/shower: Where tub meets tile, around fixtures
  • Sinks: Where sink meets countertop
  • Toilets: Base where toilet meets floor (some debate on this—it can hide leaks)
  • Windows: Interior trim gaps (use paintable caulk)
  • Baseboards: Top edge where meets wall
  • Kitchen: Backsplash to counter joint
Stop and Call a Pro If...
  • There's evidence of water damage behind the caulk (soft drywall, mold, rot)
  • Tiles are loose or damaged
  • Grout between tiles is failing extensively
  • You're dealing with a fiberglass shower unit that's cracked
  • The gap is larger than 1/4 inch (may need backer rod first)

Renter vs. Homeowner

Renters:

  • Failed caulk should be reported to landlords—it's their responsibility to repair
  • If allowed to DIY, keep receipts for potential reimbursement
  • Document the condition before and after if you do the repair yourself

Homeowners:

  • Add annual caulk inspection to your maintenance calendar
  • Re-caulk proactively before complete failure
  • Consider professional re-caulking when selling your home for best appearance

Action Items

  1. Inspect caulk around your tub/shower, sinks, and windows
  2. Note any areas that are cracked, moldy, or failing
  3. If repairs are needed, purchase the appropriate caulk type
  4. Practice on a small, less visible area first
